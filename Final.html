Character Death Prediction

Gender and Nobility

A huge component of the Game of Thrones series is the sustainability of life in the characters. No one seems to be safe from the grave no matter what their attributes. From the total 917 named characters in the kaggle data set 191 of them have documented deaths in the first five books. This is about 22%, which is unheard of for any series. I wanted to take a closer look at to why this is. I thought that some factors like Gender, Nobility, and Allegiance could have an effect on their death probability. I also took a glance at their chapters they stayed alive in the book. Or in other words, how long after they were introduced did they die?

First, looking at the two binary factors, Nobility and Gender, we can get a decent picture of how they affect someone’s death probability. In both the graphs on These statistics on the gender can be a little skewed as their are about 4 times as many men named and documented as women. Although there is a pretty significant difference with around 10% between men (Gender=1) and women (Gender=0). Although, nobility has even more impact on the situation. Another good thing about this factor is that it is split close to in half with 430 noble person (Nobility=1) and 487 non-noble (Nobility=0). There is an obvious trend that if you are non-noble you have a better chance of not making the cut to the next chapter. There isn’t a ton of difference on either boxplots except the means for chapters alive of the side that had a higher percentage dying is lower than the contrary. The last factor that was offered in the kaggle dataset was the allegiance of each person.

Allegiance

The last factor that was offered in the kaggle dataset was the allegiance of each person. Although, there is a difference between a house and name. For example, an allegiance can be to the name Tully which would be royalty in that family or to House Tully which would be to the whole kingdom of the Tully House. There are so many allegiances to where the allegiance of an individual will most likely affect the probability of their death.

The first one we will look at is a linear model and how each factor affects the probability of a person with certain attributes dying.

Interactive models make no difference and just add more complexity to the model, so these are avoided in this case. As we can see there are certain houses that have less percentage of dead people and they have a better chance to live throughout the books and longer. Such as the Wildlings, House Baratheon, and House Greyjoy have the largest negative impact on living. Although, living and how long someone lives are not necessarily correlated. Also, there is an interesting upward trend on the later a character is introduced the higher chance they have to live. Again, our early exploratory analysis showed that the Gender and Nobility variables will affect their living probability accordingly.

The next model that should show a good prediction is a regression tree. This regression tree is going to take into account all 4 of the variables mentioned in the previous model and build a tree. This tree will go through the several factors and weigh how much each one affects the outcome and make factors more prevalent if they influence the end result. This regression tree will follow how many people that it splits and the probability of life in each step. The same is true for the regression tree for the amount of chapters the individual will sustain life.

After looking at these trees we can see that Allegiance and Intro.Chapter both have the most separation steps. To give a number to how much each factor influences the end result we make a gradient boosted tree. To do this, the program takes many trees (I used 5000) to create a forest, using different combinations of the factors. After this many iterations it should become obvious which factors take are important to the characters future. The first tree does not have the variable Intro.Chapter in it and the second tree does. Then the last tree shows the chapters alive prediction. The first gradient boosted tree shows the model from the second tree. Then the second gradient boosted tree is from the chapters alive model. Gender and Nobility really don’t have a big impact on death or the chapters that a charaacter is going to survive. We could have made this conclusion from the sparsity of these two factors in the trees, however it is nice to have a number to compare.

Thus, through this exploration of how factors affect the status of a character being dead or not, there were some useful conclusions. First, the big thing is when a character is introduced in the books. If they are introduced later their probability for being alive goes up about 0.3% for each chapter. Next, the allegiance that they belong to really affects their survival, considering there are some houses or allegiances that take heavy casualties. Also, factors like gender and nobility don’t have too much impact on someone’s survival rate.

Book vs Show

For as long as I can remember there has been comparisons made between books and the movies that have come out of them. In this specific instance, the first book in the Song of Fire and Ice series, “Game of Thrones”, can be seen as very similar to its tv counterpart. The first season in the Game of Thrones television series is suppose to match pretty well with the first book by George R.R. Martin. The goal is to take an overall perspective, instead of a scenario by scenario perspective, like which scenes are in or out of the book or show. For an overall perspective, the focus is on sentiment of each or the total feeling of each, perspectives of main characters, and similarities between words used in both.

Sentiment

The first thing that would help compare these two would be the plot sentiment arcs. However, the book is written in different perspectives for each chapter. Thus trying to line these up with what is happening in the show is nearly impossible. Therefore, with sentiment arcs out of the picture, we still turn to sentiment from the nrc dictionary. This dictionary takes each word and categorizes it into one of ten categories (Anger, Anticipation, Disgust, Fear, Joy, Negative, Positive, Sadness, Surprise, and Trust).

It can be seen that there is not a huge difference between these words from the book to that of the movie besides trust. Although every other sentiment category is under 2%, which is very good. Thus, the overall feeling of the book is very close to the show.

The sentiment attached to characters is also important. To simulate this, if the character was mentioned we took 6 words around their name (3 before mention, 3 after). Then, taking the sentiment of these words and doing the same as what we did on the book we can find an overall perception of each character. The graphs for Tyrion Lannister and Ned Stark tell us that there are differences in perception. Ned is for some reason extremely negatively perceived in the show in comparison to the book. However, Tyrion is very close for both facets.

Word Frequency

They are very similar and there is not a lot of major differences. However, to actually have a numerical significance in difference of the two regarding words, we can use term frequency.

Comparing the two documents using raw word count would not be useful at all considering the large disparity of actual content between the book and the show. The book has so much more actual text than the scripts. Therefore, we use term frequency and inverse document frequency. Term frequency is the logarithm of the word count. Inverse document frequency takes the logarithm of the the amount of documents containing the word divided by the total number of documents being examined. Then we multiply these two numbers. The terms with the highest tf-idf score will be words or people that are not mentioned much in one of the documents but is mentioned a fair amount in the other.

After taking a look at both the tf-idf scores for people and words, there is not a lot of difference in either. There are no significant characters mentioned in either the book or the movie that is not mentioned in the contrary. The words that are not characters do not seem to have any significance with them either.

The show is getting the general feel of the series through sentiment and through characters somewhat. The perception of characters is the only thing that really was a disagreement with the two being the same. There were really no discrepancies regarding words within each either. Thus, they are not leaving out important characters or story plots. Overall, the show is doing a pretty good job, at least in the first season.
